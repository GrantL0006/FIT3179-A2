What Is Electricity Consumption?

The electricity consumption per capita is an important measure of a country's electric power consumption, it is strongly tied to the economic growth from a sustainable development perspective. The electricity consumption per capita is calculated based on the total electricity generations in the country from all sources, such as power plants, solar plants â€¦ etc., and subtracted the power losses in the distribution process such as electricity transmissions. The net electricity production in the country is divided by the mid-year population on the country, indicating the average electricity consumed by everyone. It is important to understand the energy consumption does not only reflect the amount of electricity usage by the population, but also show the development of a country. The electricity consumption is a sub-section of energy consumption, which includes not only the energy requires to live comfortably but is also includes the electricity consumptions to operate transportations and factories.

Electricity Carbon Emissions

Carbon emission is the major byproduct of electricity generation and is a matter that should not be neglected. It is found that the electricity production contributes to 25% of the total greenhouse gas emissions. While electric power generation is the second largest source of carbon emission. These carbon emissions from electric power plants are commonly due to the burning of coal, fossil fuels, oils, and gases. As such, countries with high electricity consumption generally would consequently have a larger carbon emission. Although solar and wind electricity generation can have less carbon emission, the efficiency of these two methods at producing electricity is far lower than traditional methods. On the left is a heat map that depicts the carbon emission of top 64 countries in the world since 1990 to 2018 sorted in alphabetical order. The heat map provides the ability to quickly identify which country produces high carbon emission and observing whether these countries reduce or increases carbon emission.

The below bar chart depicts the top 10 countries with highest electricity usage, where Iceland has an electricity consumption that is more than double than the second highest country, Norway. While the pie chart depicting the distribution of electricity generation methods globally in 2019. Renewables and gas are the largest two methods used in electricity generation, whereas green energy generation methods such as solar and wind still have lowest electricity generation contribution due to their respective technological limitations.

Do Countries Produce Enough Electricity?

Not all country consumes electricity the same amount as how much they are capable in producing. Some countries rely on importation from other countries to have enough resources for electricity production. While some countries have abundant natural resources to produce electricity, which enable them to export these resources to other countries in exchange of economic benefits. The Energy Consumption and Production scatterplot investigates the countries' capability to self-sufficiently produce electricity. As such, a parameter known as Energy Ratio is defined in the plot. The above plot is presented in a log-log scale and an interactive feature to explore the variations in countries electrical consumption and production.

Energy ratio is calculated by dividing total national electricity consumption by the total national electricity production capability. Countries with energy ratio lower than 1 meaning the country has the capability to produce more electricity than what is consumed in the nation. This implying the country has more energy resources. While countries with energy ratio above 1 meaning the country has limited resources to produce the required electrical consumption, meaning importation of energy resources are needed. For example, Australia ha energy ratio of 0.3, meaning Australia has an abundant energy source, which enable the country to export these resources to other nations.
